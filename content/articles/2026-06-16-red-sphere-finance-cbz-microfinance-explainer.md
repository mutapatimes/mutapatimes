When the Zimbabwe Stock Exchange named the financier for its new <a href="/articles/2026-06-10-zeex-tech-stack-zse-track-record.html">trade-receivables discounting platform on ZEEX</a>, the name on the announcement was not a bank or a fintech start-up. It was Red Sphere Microfinance. For many readers that prompts a simple question: who, exactly, is Red Sphere, and why does it matter that it will sit at the centre of a platform meant to get small suppliers paid faster? This is the short answer.

## A CBZ company, not an independent lender

Red Sphere Finance (Private) Limited is a wholly owned subsidiary of CBZ Holdings, one of Zimbabwe's largest banking groups. That ownership is the first thing to understand about it. Red Sphere is not a standalone microlender competing for attention on a high street; it is the group's dedicated microfinance arm, and it carries CBZ's balance sheet and risk discipline behind it. When the ZSE describes Red Sphere as the "anchor financing partner" of the receivables platform, that CBZ backing is a large part of what the word anchor is doing.

The company is licensed by the Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe as a credit-only microfinance institution under the Microfinance Act [Chapter 24:30]. The phrase credit-only matters. Zimbabwe's microfinance law recognises two kinds of institution: deposit-taking microfinance banks, which can hold the public's savings, and credit-only institutions, which cannot take deposits and instead lend out their own and their parent's capital. Red Sphere is the second kind. It is a lender, not a place to bank your money.

## What it actually does

Red Sphere began business in October 2019 and lends across a seven-town footprint that reaches well beyond the capital: Harare, Bulawayo and Mutare, but also Marondera, Chiredzi, Zvishavane and Masvingo. Its stated purpose is the standard microfinance mission of driving financial inclusion and economic development, and it pursues it through two broad lines: consumer loans to individuals, and productive loans to micro, small and medium enterprises. That MSME lending is the thread that connects its day job to its new role on ZEEX.

It is also an established name in the sector rather than a new entrant. In a 2020 access-to-finance study by the Netherlands development organisation SNV, Red Sphere was already on the list of microfinance institutions surveyed, and was noted for its experience using young people as agents in its delivery channel. That is a useful tell: a lender comfortable building networks of agents to reach customers is a lender thinking about distribution, which is exactly the muscle a national receivables platform will need.

## Why it was chosen for ZEEX

The trade-receivables discounting platform is, in plain terms, a marketplace where a small supplier who is owed money on an invoice can get most of that cash now, rather than waiting 30 or 60 days, by selling the invoice at a small discount to a financier. The ZSE provides the regulated venue and the technology; someone has to provide the money and decide which invoices are good. That someone is Red Sphere.

Its general manager, Desmund Ali, framed the company's job as twofold: providing the anchor financing, and "supporting prudent risk assessment based on counterparty creditworthiness." In other words, Red Sphere both funds the early payment and judges the risk, weighing not just the supplier but the buyer who ultimately has to settle the invoice. That is recognisably a credit institution's core skill, applied to a new product. The platform's headline promise, disbursement within 48 hours on approved 30-day and 60-day invoices, only works if a financier with capital and a credit-assessment process stands behind it.

The choice is also a statement of intent. The ZSE could have paired its platform with a digital-lending newcomer; instead it picked the microfinance arm of a major banking group. For a market trying to convince small businesses and their buyers that receivables discounting is safe, regulated and permanent rather than experimental, an established, RBZ-licensed CBZ company is a deliberately reassuring partner. It also marks an expansion for Red Sphere itself, from individual and MSME lending into supply-chain finance delivered through an exchange.

## The bottom line

Red Sphere Finance is not a household name, but its role on ZEEX is significant. It is the capital and the credit judgement behind a platform designed to fix one of the most chronic problems Zimbabwean small businesses face: getting paid on time. Whether the platform succeeds will depend on the technology the ZSE builds and on how cheaply and quickly Red Sphere can actually turn an approved invoice into cash in a supplier's account. But the financier is now known, it is regulated, and it is backed by one of the country's largest banking groups. That is a more solid starting point than many such schemes have had.
