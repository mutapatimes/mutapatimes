In our analysis of the Zimbabwe Entrepreneurship Exchange, <a href="/articles/2026-06-09-zeex-zimbabwe-entrepreneurship-exchange-sme.html">ZEEX</a>, we argued that the real test would be the technology: whether a trader in Bulawayo or Mutare can buy a listed security from a phone, with settlement they trust, without a Harare broker and a paper certificate. This is that test, examined on its own. And the good news comes first, because there is a lot of it.

The institution building ZEEX is not a technology novice. The Zimbabwe Stock Exchange has spent the better part of a decade replacing manual processes with electronic ones, and it has done so repeatedly and, mostly, successfully. As recently as November 2025 it completed a migration to a brand-new automated trading system and central securities depository across both the ZSE and the VFEX. The plumbing ZEEX would sit on top of is, in other words, freshly modernised. That is a strong foundation, and it is the single best reason to believe ZEEX can be built on current-generation rather than legacy infrastructure.

## What a growth-market stack needs to look like

The reason the stack matters is that SME exchanges do not fail on their rulebooks. They fail on access. A market for small companies and first-time investors lives or dies on whether ordinary people can reach it cheaply, from a phone, without specialist intermediaries. The technology is not a detail beneath the strategy. It is the strategy.

The benchmark already exists in the region, and it is worth being concrete about it. On the Johannesburg Stock Exchange, platforms such as STT's Avvento Trader show what the current standard looks like: a progressive web app that installs on any smartphone or tablet, direct-market-access for clients with real-time risk management layered on top, FIX APIs for order routing, a suite of algorithms, and the whole thing white-labelled to a broker's own brand and domain. The settlement and back office are integrated rather than bolted on. That is the shape of a modern, retail-reachable market: mobile-first, low-friction, and built so a client never has to touch a paper certificate or a phone call to a broker.

For ZEEX, the equivalent checklist writes itself. Mobile-money integration with EcoCash and OneMoney so investors can fund and settle in the rails they already use. Settlement at T+2 or better, on electronic records. Digital onboarding that does not require a full stockbroking relationship. Simplified, machine-readable disclosure suited to a company with 20 staff rather than 2,000. Get those right and ZEEX reaches a public the Main Board's institutional plumbing never has. Miss them, and it becomes a smaller, quieter version of a market that small investors already find hard to enter.

## The one piece still missing

Here is the honest gap, and it is the part to keep watching. For all the encouraging history, the ZSE has not said what ZEEX will actually run on. We do not yet know whether ZEEX will ride on the new November 2025 trading and depository systems, whether it will be mobile-first or desktop-bound, whether it will integrate mobile money, or how onboarding and settlement will work for a first-time retail investor. Those answers will tell us more about ZEEX's chances than any line in the approval announcement.

There is one cautionary note in the record worth holding alongside the optimism. The trading engine has been modernised again and again, but the customer-facing layer has not always kept pace, as the 2016 website saga showed, when the public site went dark and a replacement took a tender and time to arrive. An SME exchange is almost all customer-facing layer. The institution that has repeatedly nailed the engine now has to nail the part it has historically found harder.

None of that dims the case for optimism. It sharpens the question. The ZSE has the rare advantage of building ZEEX immediately after refreshing its core systems, with a decade of modernisation muscle behind it and a chief executive who has run exchange technology at scale. The foundations are as good as they have ever been. What remains is for the exchange to show its working: to publish the stack, and to make it mobile-first, cheap to reach and genuinely built for the small investor. When it does, this analysis will return to mark it against the benchmark above. On the evidence of the last ten years, there is good reason to expect it can clear the bar. The task now is simply to prove it.
