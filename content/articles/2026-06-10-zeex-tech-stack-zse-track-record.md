In our analysis of the Zimbabwe Entrepreneurship Exchange, <a href="/articles/2026-06-09-zeex-zimbabwe-entrepreneurship-exchange-sme.html">ZEEX</a>, we argued that the real test would be the technology: whether a trader in Bulawayo or Mutare can buy a listed security from a phone, with settlement they trust, without a Harare broker and a paper certificate. This is that test, examined on its own. And the good news comes first, because there is a lot of it.

The institution building ZEEX is not a technology novice. The Zimbabwe Stock Exchange has spent the better part of a decade replacing manual processes with electronic ones, and it has done so repeatedly and, mostly, successfully. As recently as November 2025 it completed a migration to a brand-new automated trading system and central securities depository across both the ZSE and the VFEX. Months earlier it had joined the SWIFT network and launched an AI-enabled market-data platform. The plumbing ZEEX would sit on top of is, in other words, freshly modernised. That is a strong foundation, and it is the single best reason to believe ZEEX can be built on current-generation rather than legacy infrastructure.

The current engine, by the accounts available, works. In InfoTech's own case study, the head of trading at IH Securities, Kudzai Machuma, described a system with no paper trail that is faster and more efficient, while the ZSE's ICT manager, Matthew Zungu, reported no downtime since launch. Vendor-published testimonials are not independent audits and should be read with that in mind, but they point the same way as the institutional record: when the ZSE sets out to modernise its trading engine, it tends to deliver.

## July 2025, when the ZSE joined the global rails

And the modernisation did not stop at the trading engine. In May 2025 the exchange launched Data Direct, an AI-enabled market-data platform spanning both the ZSE and the VFEX, with a view-only terminal for live prices and subscriptions paid through Paynow, the local payment gateway. That last detail matters more than it sounds: it shows the ZSE already moving real money through the mobile-friendly rails that retail investors actually use, rather than treating digital payment as a future ambition.

Two months later came the bigger signal. In July 2025 ZSE Holdings integrated with SWIFT, the global standard for secure financial messaging, taking the identifier XZIMZWHA and connecting the exchange and its depositories directly to international custodians, settlement banks and brokers. For a market that wants foreign capital to trust its plumbing, joining SWIFT is not a cosmetic upgrade. It is the difference between being on the world's financial network and being adjacent to it, and it brings faster, standardised settlement messaging and lower operational risk with it. These are not the moves of an institution standing still.

## What a growth-market stack needs to look like

The reason the stack matters is that SME exchanges do not fail on their rulebooks. They fail on access. A market for small companies and first-time investors lives or dies on whether ordinary people can reach it cheaply, from a phone, without specialist intermediaries. The technology is not a detail beneath the strategy. It is the strategy.

The benchmark already exists in the region, and it is worth being concrete about it. On the Johannesburg Stock Exchange, platforms such as STT's Avvento Trader show what the current standard looks like: a progressive web app that installs on any smartphone or tablet, direct-market-access for clients with real-time risk management layered on top, FIX APIs for order routing, a suite of algorithms, and the whole thing white-labelled to a broker's own brand and domain, hosted in an ISO 27001-certified, exchange-colocated environment with disaster recovery. The settlement and back office are integrated rather than bolted on. That is the shape of a modern, retail-reachable market: mobile-first, low-friction, and built so a client never has to touch a paper certificate or a phone call to a broker.

For ZEEX, the equivalent checklist writes itself. Mobile-money integration with EcoCash and OneMoney so investors can fund and settle in the rails they already use. Settlement at T+2 or better, on electronic records. Digital onboarding that does not require a full stockbroking relationship. Simplified, machine-readable disclosure suited to a company with 20 staff rather than 2,000. Get those right and ZEEX reaches a public the Main Board's institutional plumbing never has. Miss them, and it becomes a smaller, quieter version of a market that small investors already find hard to enter.

## More than an exchange: an SME toolkit

There is a wider point here that reframes ZEEX entirely. The ZSE is not building a single SME product; it is assembling an SME toolkit. In May 2026, weeks before the ZEEX approval, ZSE Holdings issued a request for proposals for an invoice and trade-receivables discounting platform, a system to let small suppliers monetise approved invoices early by auctioning them to competing financiers. Equity capital on ZEEX and working capital through receivables discounting are two halves of the same problem: small businesses cannot grow without funding, and the ZSE is now building for both.

The specification of that tender is, in its own right, the clearest window we have into the standard the exchange now sets. It asks for a cloud-hosted system with documented disaster recovery, RESTful, FIX and SWIFT-ready APIs, integration with credit reference bureaus and accounting packages such as Sage, Pastel and Xero, multi-factor authentication, AES-256 encryption, and a responsive web and mobile interface with email and SMS notifications. We do not yet have the ZEEX stack in writing, but that is the bar the institution is writing into its own contracts, and it is a modern one. The same month, ZSE Holdings also began offering paid ICT integration services to outside parties, billing engineers from US$125 an hour up to US$300 for senior work. The exchange is increasingly behaving like a technology company that happens to run markets.

## The one piece still missing

Here is the honest gap, and it is the part to keep watching. For all the encouraging history, the ZSE has not said what ZEEX will actually run on. We do not yet know whether ZEEX will ride on the new November 2025 trading and depository systems, whether it will be mobile-first or desktop-bound, whether it will integrate mobile money, or how onboarding and settlement will work for a first-time retail investor. Those answers will tell us more about ZEEX's chances than any line in the approval announcement.

One concrete clue did surface. In a 10 June 2026 partnership announcement with the asset manager INVESCI, the parties described ZEEX as a single integrated venue for primary fundraising, asset tokenisation and secondary trading, explicitly bringing tokens and invoice-discounting instruments into the ecosystem. Tokenisation is a real technical commitment, not a small one. Done well it can cut the cost of issuing and transferring small-company securities and make fractional, mobile-friendly ownership possible, which is exactly the kind of access an SME market needs. Done as a buzzword, it is just a label on a slide. Which of the two it becomes depends, once again, on the stack the ZSE has not yet published.

There are two cautionary notes in the record worth holding alongside the optimism. The first is that the trading engine has been modernised again and again, but the customer-facing layer has not always kept pace, as the 2016 website saga showed, when the public site went dark and a replacement took a tender and time to arrive. An SME exchange is almost all customer-facing layer. The institution that has repeatedly nailed the engine now has to nail the part it has historically found harder.

The second is that even a modern stack is only as reliable as its weakest link. In August 2025, trades due to settle on the 18th and 19th did not, after technical problems at the exchange's settlement bank held up the batches. The ZSE was candid about it and cleared the backlog once the bank's system was restored, but the episode makes the point plainly: a market can run a new trading engine, a SWIFT connection and an AI data platform and still stumble on a dependency it does not fully control. ZEEX will inherit that same chain, and the smaller, first-time investors it is meant to serve are precisely the ones least able to absorb a settlement that does not arrive on time.

None of that dims the case for optimism. It sharpens the question. The ZSE has the rare advantage of building ZEEX immediately after refreshing its core systems, with a decade of modernisation muscle behind it and a chief executive who has run exchange technology at scale. The foundations are as good as they have ever been. What remains is for the exchange to show its working: to publish the stack, and to make it mobile-first, cheap to reach and genuinely built for the small investor. When it does, this analysis will return to mark it against the benchmark above. On the evidence of the last ten years, there is good reason to expect it can clear the bar. The task now is simply to prove it.
