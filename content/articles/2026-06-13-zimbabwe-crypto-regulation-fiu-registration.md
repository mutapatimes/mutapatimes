Zimbabwe has introduced its first dedicated rules for the cryptocurrency sector, requiring businesses that deal in virtual assets to register with the state and pay an annual fee, or risk criminal prosecution. Under regulations issued by the Finance Minister, Mthuli Ncube, any business that buys, sells, transfers or safeguards virtual assets must register each year with the Financial Intelligence Unit, the anti-money-laundering body housed within the Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe. Registration costs US$500 a year, and operating without approval is now an offence.

It is a notable change of posture. For most of the past decade Zimbabwe's crypto market has run largely underground, and this is the first time the authorities have set out a comprehensive framework to bring it under formal supervision rather than simply restrict it.

## Why crypto took hold

Cryptocurrency adoption in Zimbabwe has been shaped by the country's long history of monetary instability. The hyperinflation of the late 2000s destroyed savings and pensions, and successive currency changes since have made it harder for many Zimbabweans to trust that money left in a local bank account will hold its value. That backdrop helped drive demand for Bitcoin and other digital assets as alternative stores of value and as a way to move money outside the formal system.

Remittances are part of the picture too. Zimbabweans abroad send significant sums home, and the channels for doing so can be expensive, with banks often among the costliest options, according to World Bank remittance data cited by Reuters. For some senders and recipients, crypto has offered a cheaper or faster route.

## Part of a wider African shift

Zimbabwe's move follows a broader regulatory push across the continent. South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya and Mauritius have all taken steps to regulate digital assets as crypto use grows. The scale of that growth is significant: Sub-Saharan Africa received more than US$205 billion in onchain value between July 2024 and June 2025, up 52 percent on the prior year, per Chainalysis figures reported by Reuters.

For regulators, the case for oversight rests on money-laundering concerns, fraud risk and gaps in consumer protection. For traders and businesses, a registration regime can offer something the informal market cannot: a recognised, legal footing.

## How it fits Zimbabwe's wider digital-finance push

The crypto rules arrive alongside a broader modernisation of Zimbabwe's financial plumbing. The Zimbabwe Stock Exchange has integrated with the SWIFT global messaging network and built out digital retail platforms, and its newly approved SME exchange, <a href="/articles/2026-06-09-zeex-zimbabwe-entrepreneurship-exchange-sme.html">ZEEX</a>, is described by the exchange as a venue for fundraising, secondary trading and asset tokenisation, the practice of issuing assets as digital tokens. We examined the technology behind that effort in a <a href="/articles/2026-06-10-zeex-tech-stack-zse-track-record.html">companion piece</a>.

The government has also been promoting a wider digital-economy agenda, from connectivity and digital-skills programmes to cybersecurity, an area directly relevant to a virtual-asset market where fraud and security are live concerns. Seen in that context, bringing crypto under anti-money-laundering supervision is consistent with a state trying to formalise more of its digital economy rather than push it away.

## What it means

The framework does not turn Zimbabwe into a crypto free-for-all. It places virtual-asset businesses under annual registration and AML supervision, with criminal liability for those who operate outside it. But it also marks a move away from the purely restrictive stance of 2018 toward one that acknowledges the role crypto already plays in payments, savings and cross-border transfers.

How much changes in practice will depend on enforcement, on whether the US$500 fee and registration requirements are workable for the smaller operators who make up much of the market, and on how many choose to come in from the informal sector rather than stay outside it. Those are open questions. What is clear is that, for the first time, Zimbabwe has a stated rulebook for an activity it spent years trying to keep at arm's length.
