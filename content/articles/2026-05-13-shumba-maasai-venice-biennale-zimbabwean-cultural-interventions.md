**Venice, Italy** — On Saturday 9 May, a series of Zimbabwean cultural interventions unfolded across the historic campos of Venice before culminating in a final gathering at the Zimbabwe Pavilion during the 61st Venice Biennale.

Led by Zimbabwean multidisciplinary artist Shumba Maasai through the ceremonial figure of Chief Ngondonga of Murehwa, the interventions transformed Venice's public squares into temporary communal gathering spaces inspired by the traditional Shona *dare* — a place of dialogue, storytelling, ceremony, and collective reflection.

Throughout the day, Chief Ngondonga and collaborators moved through multiple Venetian campos, stopping to engage passersby, Biennale visitors, artists, and members of the African diaspora through sound, conversation, ritual gesture, and live sonic fragments from *DARE*, Maasai's immersive audio-visual installation project.

Rather than presenting a fixed performance, the intervention unfolded as a living procession across the city. Each campo became a temporary *chivanze* — the communal open-air space found outside the traditional Shona *dare* where communities gather, elders deliberate, and visitors are received. In doing so, the project created a dialogue between Venetian civic architecture and Zimbabwean communal spatial traditions.

The intervention carried a symbolic announcement from Murehwa, Zimbabwe: the completion of a new community borehole. Presented ceremonially throughout Venice, the borehole became more than infrastructure — it was framed as a statement of continuity, resilience, and collective ambition.

As crowds gathered in the Venetian squares, the sound world of *DARE* echoed through the city's alleyways and canals, creating moments of reflection and unexpected connection between African ancestral traditions and contemporary global art audiences.

The day's interventions culminated in a final gathering at the Zimbabwe Pavilion at the Venice Biennale, where artists, curators, diaspora creatives, and international visitors came together in celebration and discussion. The pavilion became the closing site of the day-long journey, bringing the spirit of the campo interventions directly into Zimbabwe's official national presentation.

The Zimbabwe Pavilion, participating in the 61st Venice Biennale, continues the country's growing presence on the international contemporary art stage. This year's wider Biennale has seen African artists and pavilions occupy an increasingly significant position within global cultural conversations around identity, migration, memory, and futurity.

For many attendees, the interventions stood out for their fluidity and openness. Venice itself became part of the artwork: its public squares transformed into spaces of encounter, reunion, and exchange.

The project also highlighted the growing role of independent African-led cultural interventions operating alongside official Biennale programming. By occupying public space through movement, sound, and ceremony, the work challenged traditional boundaries between exhibition, performance, and lived communal experience.

Documented through photography and film, the interventions marked a significant moment for Zimbabwean contemporary practice in Venice — one that connected ancestral knowledge systems with contemporary artistic language while inviting audiences into a shared communal space rooted in dialogue, presence, and collective imagination.
