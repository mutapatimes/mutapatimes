The most striking number in the Department for Business and Trade's latest UK–Zimbabwe Trade and Investment Factsheet, released on 14 May 2026, is not a goods number. It is services. UK exports of services to Zimbabwe fell 19.8% in the year to December 2025, a drop of £23 million from £116 million to £93 million. UK imports of services from Zimbabwe fell 37%, from £27 million to £17 million. Together that is a £33 million contraction in services trade between the two countries in a single year — a number comparable in scale to the total Zimbabwean fruit, mineral, jewellery and art exports to the UK combined.

This is the line on which the bilateral relationship is moving fastest, and it is moving in the wrong direction.

## What the headline numbers say

The factsheet sets out the year-on-year position:

- UK services **exports** to Zimbabwe: £93 million, down 19.8% (a £23 million fall)
- UK services **imports** from Zimbabwe: £17 million, down 37% (a £10 million fall)
- The services trade surplus held by Britain: £76 million, down from £89 million a year earlier — a contraction of £13 million
- Total UK–Zimbabwe trade: £299 million, down 7.7%

The services surplus is the single line keeping the UK's overall trade balance with Zimbabwe roughly in equilibrium. The £79 million goods deficit is, almost exactly, offset by the £76 million services surplus. If the services surplus continues to shrink at the current rate, the UK will have a clear trade deficit with Zimbabwe by the end of this year.

## What the factsheet does not show

The most consequential thing about the services number is what is missing. The DBT factsheet contains a section titled "Trade in services by service type, in current prices (ONS)." For the UK–Zimbabwe corridor in the year to Q4 2025, that section reads, in full: *"Service type data for the trade in services between the UK and Zimbabwe in the four quarters to the end of Q4 2025 are not available from UK sources."*

That is the most important sentence in the document. It means the £23 million collapse in UK services exports to Zimbabwe, and the £10 million collapse in services going the other way, cannot be broken down into travel, transport, business services, financial services, insurance, telecoms, IP, or government services. The relationship's most dynamic line — the one that is changing fastest, and that has the largest UK surplus — is also the one for which DBT cannot produce a categorical breakdown for this country.

This is, in the language of the wider ONS bulletin, a structural feature, not a one-off. The ONS, in its parallel *UK Trade: March 2026* statistical bulletin released the same day, notes that monthly trade-in-services figures for the latest quarter are forecast using time series and additional sources, with revisions to be made when ITIS (the International Trade in Services) survey data lands. Travel services data have been forecast since Q3 2024 following the International Passenger Survey transformation. Smaller corridors like Zimbabwe are precisely the ones for which the underlying detail thins out fastest.

## What the wider UK picture suggests

The 14 May ONS bulletin gives some context. In Q1 2026, UK services exports overall fell by £0.7 billion, with the largest single drop coming from travel services (also £0.7 billion). Travel services are now the single category dragging on UK services exports globally. Zimbabwe is, by every reasonable read, inside that picture: the UK services exports to Zimbabwe that have fallen most are likely to be travel-related (the Zimbabwean diaspora's return-flight spend, business travel to Harare and Victoria Falls, the post-COVID rebuild of the travel-services category), with a possible secondary drag from financial and business services.

The factsheet also flags, in its mode-of-supply section, that "data on services exports to Zimbabwe by mode of supply are not available from this source" and the same for imports. Mode of supply matters because it tells you whether the services are being delivered remotely (Mode 1), through people travelling (Modes 2 and 4) or through commercial presence (Mode 3). All four modes are blacked out for Zimbabwe.

## A counter-signal in the market-share data

The one slightly positive line in the services story is market share. The UK's share of all services Zimbabwe imports rose 2.4 percentage points to 8.2% in 2024. That means the UK is shrinking less quickly than other services suppliers to Zimbabwe. Britain is taking a larger slice of a smaller pie.

By contrast the UK goods market share in Zimbabwe fell 0.1 percentage points to 0.7%. The UK position in Zimbabwe is, paradoxically, more secure in services than in goods, even as the absolute services trade contracts. That tells you something about the structural advantages the UK still has in this corridor — language, legal system, finance, professional services, education — and about how much room there is for the relationship to be rebuilt on that base, if both governments choose to do so.

## What to watch

The next factsheet is due on 23 June 2026. The June release will almost certainly include revised services numbers as more ITIS survey responses come in. The shape of those revisions — whether the 19.8% decline holds or softens, whether the services-type breakdown is reinstated for Zimbabwe — will tell us whether the current freefall is real or an artefact of the data-collection thinning out at the country-corridor level.

If it is real, it is the biggest story in the UK–Zimbabwe trade relationship this year. If it is a statistical mirage, that is its own problem: it means we are running country-by-country trade policy on numbers that DBT itself cannot break down.
