Air Zimbabwe Returns to London: Inside the Deal That Reopens the Harare Route

On 1 July, an Airbus A330 lifts off from Harare for London under the

Long Read · Business By Tendai Kuwanda June 7, 2026 9 min read

The Mutapa Times

For the first time in over a decade, you will soon be able to book a flight from Harare to London that carries the Air Zimbabwe code. On 1 July, a wide-body Airbus A330 is scheduled to lift off from Robert Gabriel Mugabe International Airport bound for the United Kingdom, several times a week, on a route the national carrier last flew with any consistency before its fleet was grounded in 2012. After years of false starts, the country's flag is going back on the long-haul map. This is how it happened, and why there is real reason for optimism this time.

The mechanism is a quiet, clever piece of aviation engineering called a wet-lease. Air Zimbabwe has signed a 13-month ACMI agreement with Plus Ultra Líneas Aéreas, a Spanish long-haul operator, brokered by Chapman Freeborn, one of Europe's leading aviation brokers. ACMI stands for Aircraft, Crew, Maintenance and Insurance: Plus Ultra supplies all four, and the flights operate under Air Zimbabwe's flight code. In plain terms, the aeroplane and the people flying it are Spanish, the badge on the ticket is Zimbabwean, and the route is Harare to London once again.

The clever bit: a legal bridge over the ban

Here is the detail that makes this deal work where so many revival announcements have failed. We went to the source document. The UK Air Safety List, in its version effective 31 October 2025, still names the carrier in black and white: "Air Zimbabwe (Pvt)", air operator's certificate number 177/04, ICAO designator AZW, banned from operating into, out of or within the United Kingdom. As recently as last autumn, in other words, a direct London service on Air Zimbabwe's own certificate was not legally possible.

But the very same document contains a deliberate door, and it is worth quoting. An air carrier subject to an operating ban, it reads, "could be permitted to exercise traffic rights to the United Kingdom… by using wet-leased aircraft of an air carrier which is not subject to an operating ban, provided that the relevant safety standards are complied with." That single sentence is the legal foundation of the entire deal.

Plus Ultra is exactly that operator. It is a Spanish, European-regulated airline, fully cleared to fly into the UK and EU. By flying Plus Ultra metal, with Plus Ultra crews, under Plus Ultra's maintenance and insurance, Air Zimbabwe gets its passengers and its brand back to London without waiting years for its own safety oversight to be re-rated. It is not a loophole so much as a designed pathway, and Air Zimbabwe is the latest of several African flag carriers to use it.

A modern aircraft on a demanding route

The corridor will be flown by an Airbus A330, the wide-body twin-aisle that Plus Ultra builds its long-haul fleet around. The flight time between Harare and London is roughly ten and a half hours, well within the A330's comfortable range. Plus Ultra's most recent A330-300 seats just over 300 passengers in two cabins, with a dedicated business class and an economy cabin with generous pitch.

The A330 matters for reasons beyond comfort. It is one of the most widely operated wide-bodies in the world, with a large global support base, which is precisely what you want underneath a route that has to run reliably for thirteen months and beyond. For travellers, it means a quiet, current cabin with modern entertainment and air filtration. For the airline, it means proven economics on a long, thin, high-value route.

Who is Plus Ultra, and why this is credible

Plus Ultra Líneas Aéreas is a Spanish carrier founded in 2012 that began operations in 2015 and is built around the A330. Its ACMI division, launched the same year, has since operated on behalf of more than one hundred airlines across Europe, the Americas, Asia and Africa. This is not a first-time experiment.

The Harare deal is the company's third long-term ACMI contract with a national flag carrier in twelve months, following agreements with Air Algérie and Tunisair. That track record is the strongest signal that this is a serious, repeatable operating model rather than another hopeful headline. Plus Ultra makes its living lending reliable long-haul capacity to national airlines, and Zimbabwe has now hired exactly that.

The paper trail: this was months in the making

The announcement landed on 5 June, but our research indicates the groundwork was laid far earlier. Chapman Freeborn Aviation Services DMCC, the Dubai-based broker named in the deal, registered as a foreign supplier on Zimbabwe's official procurement portal, the Procurement Regulatory Authority of Zimbabwe (PRAZ, at praz.org.zw), on 6 March 2026. That is nearly three months before the contract was made public, and almost four months before the first flight. Brokers do not register on a country's government tender system on a whim. It is the kind of quiet, procedural step a firm takes when a mandate is already in motion.

That single timestamp reframes the story. This is not the hurried, hopeful announcement Air Zimbabwe has made before and failed to deliver. It is the visible end of a process that had been working through the paperwork since at least the first quarter of the year, steered by an internationally recognised, ISTAT-registered broker. The firm's registered address, Office 2503 in Jumeirah Bay Tower 2 in Dubai's JLT district, places it inside the Gulf brokerage corridor that has quietly rewired long-haul African aviation, the same corridor behind Plus Ultra's recent deals with Air Algérie and Tunisair.

And a note from our reading of the fleet. Plus Ultra builds its long-haul operation on the A330, and the June agreement specifies a wide-body A330. The carrier's two A330-300s, the newest jets in its fleet and added only in late 2025, are the most likely candidates to wear the Air Zimbabwe code on this corridor. If you board the Harare to London service this winter, there is a strong chance the aircraft under you was flying Latin American routes out of Madrid a season earlier.

Why it matters more than one route

London is not just another dot on the map for Zimbabwe. The United Kingdom is home to one of the largest Zimbabwean diaspora communities in the world, and the Harare–London link is, in the most practical sense, a family route: it carries people home for weddings and funerals, students to and from university, traders, nurses, investors and tourists. Restoring a direct, flag-coded service shortens that distance for hundreds of thousands of people.

The economics point the same way. Continental research on opening up African aviation, through the Single African Air Transport Market and the Yamoussoukro Decision, estimates that fuller liberalisation could add hundreds of thousands of passengers, tens of millions of dollars in fare savings, and thousands of jobs for a country like Zimbabwe, with measurable gains in tourism, trade and GDP. A working long-haul route is the kind of connective tissue that those numbers are built on.

The honest caveats, and why the optimism still holds

Optimism is not amnesia. Air Zimbabwe's recent history is a long account of grounded fleets, unpaid debts and routes that opened and closed within months. Anyone who has followed the airline has earned the right to be cautious, and the deal is for thirteen months, not forever.

But the structure of this agreement is the reason to be hopeful rather than wary. By wet-leasing, the airline does not have to find the capital to buy a wide-body, the engineers to maintain it, or the safety re-rating to fly it into Europe tomorrow. It rents reliability, sells seats, and rebuilds the route's commercial case month by month. If the corridor performs, it becomes the foundation for the airline's own return to long-haul on its own terms. On 1 July, for the first time in a long time, the runway ahead of Air Zimbabwe points up.

The economics: what the London route could actually earn

So what is a London route worth? Here is a deliberately simple, back-of-the-envelope model. These are illustrative estimates, not Air Zimbabwe's accounts, and they measure gross passenger revenue, not profit. The ACMI lease, fuel, taxes and airport charges all come out before anything reaches the bottom line.

The assumptions are grounded in public figures: a Plus Ultra A330-300 carrying about 302 seats (30 business, 272 economy), a Harare to London distance of roughly 5,150 miles, and three return flights a week. Fares come from today's market, where one-way seats on connecting itineraries currently run from about £325 to £436 and returns from about £591 to £681. We assume a business-class seat sells for roughly three times the economy fare.

Base case: about £34mGross passenger revenue across the 13-month deal

At an 80% load factor and a typical £350 economy fare, a single A330 grosses roughly £100,000 on each one-way flight. Run three return flights a week and that is about £600,000 a week, or £2.6m a month. The implied passenger yield is close to 8 pence per mile, which converts to roughly 10 US cents and sits squarely inside the 8 to 15 cents typical of international routes.

Conservative: about £25mThin loads, cheapest fares, still firmly profitable territory

Even at a lean 65% load factor on the cheapest £325 seats, each flight still clears about £75,000, which is roughly £450,000 a week and £2.0m a month. The floor under this route is high.

Strong: about £47mPeak-season demand at full fares over the deal

Fill 90% of the cabin at peak £430 fares and each flight grosses around £140,000, or about £840,000 a week and £3.6m a month. A second daily rotation or a fourth weekly frequency would push the ceiling higher still.

All figures gross passenger revenue, illustrative, and built on a 302-seat A330 over roughly 5,150 miles. They exclude the ACMI lease cost, fuel, taxes and airport fees. The point is the order of magnitude: even cautiously, a well-run Harare to London A330 is a multi-million-pound line of business. The prize is real, which is exactly why the route has been worth fighting back to.
