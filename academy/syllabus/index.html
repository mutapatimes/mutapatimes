Objectives

What you will be able to do

Every objective is practical. By the end you should be able to:

01

Spot and shape a story

Recognise what makes a story newsworthy, tell news from opinion and PR, and find a fresh angle.

02

Report and verify

Find sources, run an interview, and verify what you are told before it becomes a fact.

03

Write and edit

Structure a piece with the inverted pyramid, write clear active copy, then fact-check and copy-edit your own work.

04

Work with the media

Pitch a story to the right journalist, deliver a sharp elevator pitch, and write a professional press release.

05

Understand the business

Explain how news is funded, including how advertising and affiliate tracking work, and read the modern audience.

06

Measure and get hired

Measure whether your work landed, build a CV and portfolio, and earn a real byline.

How it works

Learn it, then prove it.

Every lesson opens with short teaching cards, often with a chart, then a set of interactive exercises. You must pass each lesson at 80 percent before the next unlocks, so progress reflects mastery.

  • Multiple choice, sorting, matching, fill-the-gap, swipe and chart tasks.
  • Sixteen checkpoint exams and a final exam, all at 80 percent.
  • The Reading Room is compulsory before your certificate unlocks.
  • A capstone article read by a real editor.

The modules

All 18 modules, in order

From the foundations of news to getting your work published, with two brand-new units on the media world and the business of news.

1. Foundations

What news is, how it differs from opinion and PR, voice, and the ethics that hold it all up.

2. Inside the newsroom

Who does what, how a story moves from tip to publication, and how to read an outlet.

3. How news makes money

Who pays for journalism, how paywalls work, and why funding shapes the news.

4. Covering places fairly

How coverage can stereotype a place, and how to tell fuller, fairer stories.

5. A reporter's map

Regions, media hubs, languages and the bodies that shape what gets reported.

6. Reporting

Find sources, run an interview, verify what you are told, and report from a distance.

7. Writing

Structure a piece, open it well, write clearly, and edit your own copy.

8. Fact-checking and copy

The last lines of defence before a reader sees your work: verify, then catch every error.

9. Pitch and publish

Pitch a story properly, take edits well, and finish a publishable piece.

10. The digital journalist

Work across platforms, build a personal brand, and freelance with a portfolio.

11. Getting hired

Build a CV and cover letter that get a journalist noticed.

12. Going independent

Take everything you have learned and launch your own newsletter.

13. Solution journalism

Report the problem and the evidenced response to it, while still scrutinising.

New

14. Working with the media world

How journalists think, how to pitch, the elevator pitch, and the anatomy of a press release.

New

15. The business of news

Who really reads, how advertising and affiliate links work, and how to measure impact. Includes a hands-on task: break down a live advert's link.

16. Journalism jobs

What the trade looks like in a mature media market, as a benchmark.

17. Real-world critique

Read real press releases, find the story, and avoid being a stenographer.

18. Final assessment

One comprehensive exam across the whole course. Pass it, finish the Reading Room, earn your certificate.

New and hands-on

See how the money really works.

The newest units go beyond theory. You will read the modern audience in charts, then do something most courses never teach.

  • Open a real advert, paste its link, and break down every tracking and affiliate parameter.
  • See exactly how a publisher earns a commission from a click.
  • Read the audience: trust, news avoidance, platforms and who actually pays.
  • Pitch, the elevator pitch, and the press release, the way working journalists use them.
